How to Track Your Daily Life Without Overcomplicating It

Most habit trackers are too complicated to stick with. Here's a simpler approach to daily tracking that actually tells you something useful.

Most people who try to track their habits quit within two weeks. Not because they lack discipline. Because the system is too complicated. Too many habits to log, too many metrics to fill in, too much friction between opening the app and getting anything useful out of it.

There's a simpler way.

The problem with complex habit trackers

Traditional habit trackers ask you to log specific behaviours — did you exercise today, did you drink enough water, did you meditate. This works for some people. For most, it becomes a chore that stops feeling meaningful almost immediately.

The deeper problem is that tracking behaviours doesn't always tell you how you're actually doing. You can hit every habit on your list and still feel exhausted and unfocused by Thursday.

A different approach: rating your day

Instead of tracking specific behaviours, LifeScore asks you to rate six areas of your life on a scale of one to seven — exercise, sleep, focus, stress, nutrition and recovery. It takes about thirty seconds.

The rating is based on how you actually feel, not whether you completed a specific task. This makes it easier to be honest and easier to stick with over time.

What patterns emerge over time

After a few weeks of daily ratings, LifeScore identifies patterns you wouldn't notice day to day. You might discover that your focus scores are consistently lower on Wednesdays, or that your stress levels spike every time your sleep score drops below four.

These patterns are more useful than a streak counter. They tell you something real about how different areas of your life affect each other.

Thirty seconds a day

The daily check-in in LifeScore takes about thirty seconds. There's no long questionnaire, no habit list to work through, no pressure to be perfect.

Just six ratings, once a day, and a clearer picture of how you're actually doing over time.
